Spain’s Restaurant Napkins Become Cultural Treasures

Today’s lesson uses a Guardian culture feature about Spain’s thin restaurant napkins and how small everyday objects can carry local identity, memory, and design history.

Level: intermediateTime: 5–10 minutes
Source article

Title: “The beauty of the useless”: Spain’s super-thin restaurant napkins are throwaway art treasures

Source: The Guardian

Published: June 18, 2026

Original article: The Guardian article link

Article Summary

The Guardian looks at Spain’s small, very thin restaurant napkins, known as servilletas, and argues that their apparent uselessness is part of their charm. Although they do not clean well, they are printed with names, drawings, and local food references that make them miniature records of everyday bar and café culture.

The article follows photographer Felipe Hernandez, who has collected and photographed these napkins for a new book. It connects the objects to broader changes in Spanish cities, where old-school bars face rising rents, tourism, and the homogenisation of restaurant design.

文章摘要

《衛報》介紹西班牙餐廳常見的薄紙巾 servilletas，指出它們雖然幾乎不好用，卻正因如此成為西班牙酒吧與咖啡館文化的一部分。這些紙巾常印有店名、插圖與地方食物線索，像是日常生活中的迷你文化紀錄。

文章也介紹攝影師 Felipe Hernandez 的新書：他多年來收集並拍攝各地餐廳紙巾。這些小物件反映出西班牙城市的變化，包括老派酒吧面臨租金上漲、觀光壓力，以及餐飲設計越來越同質化的趨勢。

5 Daily Words

flimsy

weak, thin, and easily damaged; not strong or solid.

In article: “Forever flimsy and ineffective at cleaning greasy fingers, the servilletas of the Iberian peninsula resist the relentless ‘optimisation’ of our age.”

脆弱的、薄弱的；不結實、容易破損或缺乏支撐力。

Usage note: Flimsy can describe paper, clothing, excuses, evidence, or arguments.

ubiquitous

found or seen everywhere; extremely common in a particular place or situation.

In article: “If you have ever eaten a meal in a bar, cafe or restaurant in Spain and grabbed a napkin from the ubiquitous small metal dispensers…”

無所不在的、到處可見的；在某地或某種情境中非常常見。

Usage note: This word is useful for describing common technologies, habits, products, or symbols.

patrons

customers or regular visitors of a restaurant, bar, theatre, library, or similar place.

In article: “Piling scrunched up servilletas on a plate after use may seem the logical choice, yet in some establishments patrons simply chuck them on the ground…”

顧客、常客；餐廳、酒吧、劇院、圖書館等場所的客人或固定使用者。

Usage note: Patron sounds more formal than customer and often suggests support for a place or institution.

homogenisation

the process by which different things become more similar, uniform, or standardized.

In article: “Such small visual quirks feel even more special when you consider the growing homogenisation of Spain’s gastronomic sector.”

同質化；不同事物逐漸變得相似、統一或標準化的過程。

Usage note: British spelling uses -isation; American spelling is homogenization.

gentrification

the process in which a poorer or older urban area becomes more expensive and middle-class, often pushing out existing residents or businesses.

In article: “Like many local businesses in Spanish cities, some of the places featured in Hernandez’s book have been struggling to deal with the side-effects of gentrification and tourism.”

仕紳化；較老舊或較低收入的城市區域變得更昂貴、更中產化，常使原有居民或商家被迫離開的過程。

Usage note: This is a harder urban-policy word, often used in debates about housing, tourism, and local identity.
